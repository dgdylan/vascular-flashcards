If the systemic blood pressure rises but flow remains constant, what happens to the resistance in the vascular beds?
Answer: A. increases
Feedback:
If Flow Volume (Q) remains the same, if pressure (P) changes, resistance (R) must also change to keep the same flow volume. Resistance and pressure are directly related. If pressure goes up, resistance must go up to maintain a consistent amount of flow volume. Simplified Mathematic Example: Flow Volume (Q) 6 = (P) 12 / (R) 2 Pressure goes up to 24, Resistance must increase to 4 to calculate the same flow volume of 6. (24 / 4 = 6)

Question 56
Missed
Front
Which of the following correctly describes a normal Doppler waveform of an artery from the lower extremity?
Answer: B. The triphasic waveform will have two components of antegrade flow during the cardiac cycle.
Feedback:
The resistance in the peripheral vascular beds is very high compared to the abdominal organs. Blood flows into the extremities during systole with flow reversal during diastole. The resistance in the vascular beds is greater than the resistance in the feeding vessels during mid/end diastole causing flow reversal to occur in the vessel. This produces a waveform with at least two separate phases of flow (biphasic) If the pressure gradient between the vascular bed and the feeding vessel continues to equalize, a small amount of flow may move toward the vascular bed at the very end of diastole. This produces a waveform with three separate phases of flow (triphasic), 2 antegrade and 1 retrograde component. Biphasic and triphasic flow is generally considered normal in the extremities.
