Front
During inspiration the flow in the veins of the upper extremities _____________.
Answer: A. increases
Feedback:
During inspiration the pressure within the chest cavity decreases allowing venous flow from the upper extremities to increase. During expiration, the pressure within the chest cavity increases causing the venous flow from the upper extremities to decrease. THE OPPOSITE IS TRUE WITH THE FLOW FROM THE LOWER EXTREMITIES. Question 12
Front
When transmural pressure in a vein is high:
Answer: C. the vein becomes enlarged and rounded
Feedback:
Transmural pressure is the pressure within the veins pushing outward and is normally low. It increases with increasing venous volume. When the pressure inside the vein is high compared to the surrounding pressure outside the vein, the vessel distends. When the pressure inside the vein is low compared to the surrounding pressure outside the vein, the vessel collapses to a dumbbell shape.

Question 43
Missed
Front
What venous valves are closed in the lower extremity during calf muscle relaxation?
Answer: B. Proximal thigh veins
Feedback:
During muscle contraction, the large blood volume residing in the deep calf veins and soleal sinuses is squeezed up and out of the calf. The proximal valves of the deep veins are forced open. The distal valves close to prevent caudal flow. The perforator valves close to prevent flow into the superficial venous system. During calf muscle relaxation, the proximal valves close due to the hydrostatic pressure forcing blood back down the veins to the level of the valve. During relaxation, the distal and perforator valves open, and blood flows into the calf deep veins.

Front
Which of the following terms would be used to describe abnormal venous flow in the deep veins of the thigh?
Answer: C. cardiac pulsatility
Feedback:
The term pulsatility refers to repetitive flow changes demonstrated on the Doppler tracing. Veins closest to the heart will demonstrate cardiac pulsatility due to the changes in right atrial pressure. It can be equated to a "ripple effect", like a stone being thrown into a pool of water. The variations in flow are demonstrated in vessels further from the heart as they become engorged with blood. If venous flow is pulsatile in the lower extremities, cardiac complications like CHF or severe tricuspid regurgitation should be suspected. Normal venous flow varies with respiration and has very low velocity. Spontaneous flow should also be present in most veins in the body (without any augmentation maneuvers applied).
