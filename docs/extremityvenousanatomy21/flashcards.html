Choices
Answer: C. the vein becomes enlarged and rounded
Feedback:
Transmural pressure is the pressure within the veins pushing outward and is normally low. It increases with increasing venous volume. When the pressure inside the vein is high compared to the surrounding pressure outside the vein, the vessel distends. When the pressure inside the vein is low compared to the surrounding pressure outside the vein, the vessel collapses to a dumbbell shape.

Choices
Answer: C. cardiac pulsatility
Feedback:
The term pulsatility refers to repetitive flow changes demonstrated on the Doppler tracing. Veins closest to the heart will demonstrate cardiac pulsatility due to the changes in right atrial pressure. It can be equated to a "ripple effect", like a stone being thrown into a pool of water. The variations in flow are demonstrated in vessels further from the heart as they become engorged with blood. If venous flow is pulsatile in the lower extremities, cardiac complications like CHF or severe tricuspid regurgitation should be suspected. Normal venous flow varies with respiration and has very low velocity. Spontaneous flow should also be present in most veins in the body (without any augmentation maneuvers applied).
