Question 1

Front
At the start of an endovenous ablation procedure for the great saphenous vein, the catheter is inserted _____________________ and advanced to a position_____________________ before activating the device.
Choices
Answer: C. into the distal GSV , 2cm distal to the saphenofemoral junction
Feedback:
At the start of an endovenous ablation procedure for the GSV, the catheter is inserted into the distal vein and advanced to a position 2cm distal to the SFJ before the device is activated. Once activated, the catheter is slowly withdrawn down the leg and out through the puncture site. This should cause fibrosis of the vein in the treated area. The catheter tip must be 2cm or more from the SFJ to prevent damage to the common femoral vein.

Question 7
Front
Which of the following is an advantage of intraoperative vascular ultrasound compared to intraoperative angiography?
Choices
Answer: A. Technique does not require vessel puncture to obtain flow information
Feedback:
Angiography is an invasive procedure that requires a vessel puncture to administer contrast. Intraoperative US is performed by placing the transducer on the artery, no puncture required to obtain flow information. Both ultrasound and angiography can provide real time blood flow information because the contrast flow is evaluated live with fluoroscopy on angiography. Ultrasound does not evaluate all branches of the intracerebral vessels while angiography can visualize all patent branches in the FOV. Both techniques can be performed during the surgery.

Question 9
Front
What is the preferred patient position during catheter retraction in an endovenous laser ablation treatment?
Choices
Answer: C. Trendelenburg
Feedback:
When the catheter is inserted at the start of the procedure, the semi-erect or reverse Trendelenburg position is helpful to better visualize the advancement of the catheter. Once the laser is activated and the catheter is retracted, the patient is placed in the Trendelenburg position to reduce the blood volume in the veins of the leg and facilitate the closure of the vein during the procedure. The laser essentially damages the walls of the veins causing a reduced lumen diameter and thrombus formation. The less blood that's in the vein, the easier it will be to successfully treat the vein.

Front
When evaluating the left arm for potential placement of a central venous catheter, you identify multiple small venous collaterals surrounding the subclavian vein, What should you do next?
Choices
Answer: A. evaluate the venous system in the right arm
Feedback:
If venous collaterals are identified on the pre-procedure scan for central venous catheter placement, there is a stenosis or occlusion of the adjacent major vein. The patency and size of the collaterals are insignificant when deciding not to use that extremity for the catheter. The mere presence of the collaterals indicates an issue with that venous pathway. Normal veins are preferred for catheter placement. These findings are usually going to change the access point or placement of the central venous catheter to another vein/arm, but the Sonographer does not advise the physician on that decision. The opposite arm should be evaluated for an alternate location for catheter placement. Arteries cannot be used for the same purpose as veins for catheter delivery of medications.

You are evaluating a patient that had a balloon angioplasty on the proximal superficial femoral artery 3 years ago. The acceleration time in the common femoral artery is 230ms. This finding is most suggestive of:
Choices
Answer: D. iliac artery stenosis
Feedback:
Increased AT = longer time to reach peak velocity = lower resistance = proximal obstruction Decreased AT = less time to reach peak velocity = higher resistance = distal obstruction Increased acceleration time indicates a proximal stenosis. There is probably an iliac artery stenosis and the arteries proximal to the CFA should be evaluated. If the AT in the CFA was shorter or decreased, this indicates a stenosis distally. In this scenario the femoral artery may have restenosis or the distal SFA could have stenosis.

An AVF is a direct connection of an artery or vein caused by trauma or interventional procedure. Due to the acute formation of the connection, there are no collaterals present. The arterial inflow and venous outflow will demonstrate the same hemodynamic characteristics in an AVM and AVF, increased arterial flow volume, low resistance arterial inflow and pulsatile, turbulent venous outflow.

Choices

Feedback:
An absolute contraindication is situation which makes a particular treatment or procedure absolutely inadvisable. These include a known allergy to the injected material (ablation, steroid injection) , lack of appropriate equipment or skill to complete the procedure, uncooperative patient (uncontrolled movement during the procedure can result in injury, laceration or hemorrhage ) A relative contraindication is a condition which makes a particular treatment or procedure possibly inadvisable. Changes can be made to make the procedure safer or easier to perform. These include coagulopathy and the use of blood thinners, aspirin or antibiotics. If permitted by their physician, the patient can discontinue their blood thinner medication until their PT and INR values return to normal levels and then proceed with the invasive procedure. Warfarin, Coumadin, Jantoven, Xarelto, Pradaxa or Lovenox, Aspirin, Ibuprofen, NSAIDs should be stopped at least 7 days before a procedure and resumed 7 days after the procedure. Other relative contraindications include: Unsafe access route - must avoid large arteries and adjacent nerves Local infection, rash, or skin breakdown
