If the systemic blood pressure rises but flow remains constant, what happens to the resistance in the vascular beds?
A:increases
B:unable to determine without knowing the flow velocity
C:decreases
D:no change
Show answer and feedback
Answer: A. increases
Feedback:
If Flow Volume (Q) remains the same, if pressure (P) changes, resistance (R) must also change to keep the same flow volume. Resistance and pressure are directly related. If pressure goes up, resistance must go up to maintain a consistent amount of flow volume. Simplified Mathematic Example: Flow Volume (Q) 6 = (P) 12 / (R) 2 Pressure goes up to 24, Resistance must increase to 4 to calculate the same flow volume of 6. (24 / 4 = 6)

This produces a waveform with at least two separate phases of flow (biphasic) If the pressure gradient between the vascular bed and the feeding vessel continues to equalize, a small amount of flow may move toward the vascular bed at the very end of diastole. This produces a waveform with three separate phases of flow (triphasic), 2 antegrade and 1 retrograde component. Biphasic and triphasic flow is generally considered normal in the extremities.
